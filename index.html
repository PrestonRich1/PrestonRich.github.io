The Power of VTEC

 The phrases "Honda power" and "VTEC power" seem to be floating around in the car community, but what is all the excitement about? VTEC and i-VTEC systems are composed of two camshafts that are hydraulically controlled by the vehicle's ECU. This two camshaft system is the staple of VTEC and a statement of power by Honda.  

What Makes VTEC Special?

The ability to harness the power of two camshafts gives drivers incredible control over the timing of air intake relative to piston position. This is preferable over a single camshaft because when the engine gets to higher Rotations Per Minute (RPMs) with a single camshaft, the air intake doesn't actually adjust to the increasing rotations within the engine. Having two camshafts with VTEC essentially gives the car two settings. One adjusted for lower RPMs with the appropriate low-speed cam timings and one adjusted for higher RPMs with high-speed cam timings. This allows the intake valves to open at the right time and close when the pistons are bottomed out at almost perfect timing to provide peak efficiency for both low and high RPMs.

For More than Racing

Because a VTEC system lets the engine run smoother and more efficient at all ranges of RPMs, this gives the vehicle far better fuel economy. The low-speed camshaft allows the car to have an easy, smooth, and controlled start and the high-speed camshafts' allows the engine to run at higher power without any poor combustion timing to blow through fuel.

VTEC's History

The history of VTEC all began in 1998 with the Honda Integra XSi (or Acura Integra in North American models) and was introduced on a B16a engine. However, VTEC wasn't released in America until the 1991 mid-engine Honda NSX was released. VTEC saw many evolutions through systems such as DOHC VTEC, SOHC VTEC, and the first i-VTEC system. When the K-series VTEC came along, VTEC had made a name for itself. The K-series brought many new and powerful Honda engines with two distinct types. The first used VTEC on economy vehicles (more or less for fuel economy) but the other introduced a powerful line of K-series engines that crossed the line of 160 hp and reached all the way to 200 hp in cars with minimal weight. i-VTEC got Variable Cylinder Management and eventually i-VTEC i which included direct injection in 2004. Other series and systems such as the R-series, AVTEC, a turbo VTEC system and even VTEC in motorcycles came along.

A Conclusion of VTEC

In the car community, VTEC is known for its incredibly efficiency and power. However, this system can not fully be credited for the phrase "Honda power." The fact is, Honda's B, K, and R series engines, their methods of keeping vehicle weight low, and the incredible aerodynamics of many powerful Hondas all came together to produce these great cars. However, even if a VTEC system isn't all that it takes to make a powerful car, it is a key factor and set Honda's legacy in stone.
